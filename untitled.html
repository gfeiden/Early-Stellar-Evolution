Abstract
Absolute stellar ages are some of the most sought after astrophysical quantities. This is especially true for identifiably young stellar systems, where absolute ages provide the only constraint on time-dependent features of star and planet formation. Unfortunately, accurate absolute ages for young stars remain elusive due largely to a necessary reliance on theoretical stellar structure and evolution models. I review the canonical description of early stellar evolution as the quasi-hydrostatic collapse of a homogeneous gas sphere governed by the standard stellar structure equations, and I highlight key evidence challenging this paradigm that prevent accurate estimates of young stellar ages. I also review recent progress incorporating ``non-standard'' physics such as accretion, rotation, and magnetic fields, which help explain the observed discrepancies. However, I highlight several fundamental limitations about how these processes operate that preclude a coherent understanding about the physics required to accurately predict the properties, and thus ages, of young stars.

Protostellar Evolution

Discussion of protostellar evolution, including a simple picture (gravitational collapse mediated by thermal pressure), evidence for/against the simple picture, and potential (re: likely) complications.

Basic Picture

In the simplest view, protostars are large balls of gas collapsing under the influence of self-gravity.

Evidence

Looking at how the simple picture compares to observational evidence.
